# MILEJPSR Framework for Product Sense Questions

## What This Framework Is

MILEJPSR is a comprehensive framework for product design and improvement questions. It combines strategic context (Mission, Industry), user research (Landscape, Experience, Judge), solution generation (Propose), and business thinking (Success metrics, Recommend).

**MILEJPSR stands for:**
- **M**ission & Intent
- **I**ndustry Landscape (skip if time is short)
- **L**andscape the Ecosystem & Segment Users
- **E**xperience & Pain Points (User Journey)
- **J**udge & Prioritise Pain Points
- **P**ropose Solutions
- **S**pecify Success Metrics
- **R**ecommend

**Mnemonic:** "Miles Per Second Race"

---

## When to Use This Framework

**Use for questions like:**
- "Design a product for [User Segment]"
- "How would you improve [Product]?"
- "Build a feature to help [Users] with [Problem]"
- "If you were PM for [Product], what would you build?"
- "Design [Product Category] for [Company]"

**Core skill being tested:**
Can you think like a product leader? Strategic context → user research → prioritization → solution design → business outcomes

---

## What This Framework Tests

### Strategic Thinking
- Do you understand why the company would enter this space?
- Do you consider market size, trends, competitive landscape?
- Do you connect product decisions to company mission?

### User Empathy
- Can you segment users meaningfully (MECE)?
- Do you understand user journeys deeply?
- Can you identify real pain points (not assumed)?

### Prioritization
- Can you choose ONE segment from many?
- Can you choose ONE pain point from many?
- Can you defend your prioritization with a framework?

### Solution Design
- Can you generate multiple diverse solutions?
- Do you choose the best one with clear reasoning?
- Do you acknowledge trade-offs?

### Business Acumen
- Do you define success metrics clearly?
- Do you tie the solution to business outcomes?
- Do you show conviction in your recommendation?

## The Eight Steps (20-25 minutes total)

### Step 1: Mission & Intent (2 minutes)

**What to do:**
Establish strategic context. Why would THIS company build THIS?

**Structure:**
```
"Let me start with strategic context:

Why this company?
- [Market size]: [TAM/growth]
- [Industry trends]: [What's changing]
- [Mission alignment]: [How this fits company's purpose]
- [Unique advantage]: [What they bring that others can't]

This is a [X]B market growing [Y]% YoY. [Company] is uniquely positioned because [advantage]."
```

**Example for "Design a product for elderly to stay connected":**
```
"Let me start with strategic context:

Why Facebook?
- Market size: 50M US seniors (65+), growing as boomers age
- Industry trend: Seniors adopting smartphones (75% now have one, up from 40% in 2019)
- Mission alignment: "Bring the world closer together" — seniors are isolated
- Unique advantage: Facebook already has the family graph (grandkids, children on platform)

This is a 50M user opportunity. Facebook is uniquely positioned because they already have the relationships — they just need to make the product accessible."
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Named the company explicitly
- Quantified market size
- Mentioned a trend (not static)
- Connected to mission
- Stated unique advantage

---

### Step 2: Industry Landscape (1-2 minutes) — SKIP IF TIME IS SHORT

**What to do:**
Map the value chain and competitive landscape.

**Structure:**
```
"Quick industry landscape:

Supply side: [Who provides value]
Demand side: [Who consumes value]
Existing players: [Who else is here]
Company's position: [Where we play today]"
```

**Example:**
```
"Quick industry landscape:

Supply side: Content creators (family members posting), telecom (connectivity)
Demand side: Seniors wanting connection
Existing players: Zoom (video), GrandPad (simplified tablet), Lively (emergency devices)
Facebook's position: Already has family connections, just need senior-friendly interface"
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Mapped supply and demand
- Named 2-3 competitors
- Showed where company already has strength

**Note:** If time is tight (20 min interview), SKIP this step. Go straight to Landscape Users.

---

### Step 3: Landscape the Ecosystem & Segment Users (3 minutes)

**What to do:**
Segment users into MECE groups. Prioritize ONE using SFARU.

**Structure:**
```
"Let me segment users:

Segment 1: [Name] — [Defining behavior]
Segment 2: [Name] — [Defining behavior]
Segment 3: [Name] — [Defining behavior]

Prioritizing using SFARU:
- Size: [Which is biggest?]
- Frequency: [Which uses it most often?]
- Alignment: [Which fits mission best?]
- Revenue: [Which pays/monetizes best?]
- Underserved: [Which is most poorly served today?]

I'll focus on [Segment X] because [reason]."
```

**Example:**
```
"Let me segment elderly users by digital fluency:

Segment 1: Digitally Native Seniors (65-70, used computers at work)
- Comfortable with tech, just need better UX

Segment 2: Digital Immigrants (70-80, adopted tech in retirement)
- Can use smartphones but struggle with complexity

Segment 3: Tech Averse (80+, never adopted digital)
- Intimidated by screens, prefer analog

Prioritizing using SFARU:
- Size: Segment 2 is largest (60% of 65+ population)
- Frequency: Segment 2 would use daily if easier (currently weekly)
- Alignment: Facebook's mission is about connection — Segment 2 WANTS to connect but can't
- Revenue: Facebook makes $60/user/year on ads — this segment would be similar
- Underserved: Segment 1 can already use Facebook. Segment 3 won't adopt digital. Segment 2 is the gap.

I'll focus on Digital Immigrants (70-80) because they're the largest underserved segment with high intent but low ability."
```

**SFARU Rubric:**
- **S**ize: How many users? (largest = best)
- **F**requency: How often would they use it? (daily > weekly)
- **A**lignment: Does it fit company mission? (yes/no)
- **R**evenue: ARPU potential or monetization path? (higher = better)
- **U**nderserved: Gap between need and current solutions? (bigger gap = better)

**What evaluator looks for:**
- 3 segments defined by BEHAVIOR (not demographics)
- Segments are MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive)
- Used SFARU or similar framework to prioritize
- Picked ONE segment with clear reasoning
- Eliminated others explicitly

---

### Step 4: Experience & Pain Points (3 minutes)

**What to do:**
Map the end-to-end user journey. Identify friction at each step.

**Structure:**
```
"For [Segment], here's the journey:

Step 1: [Stage] → Pain: [What goes wrong]
Step 2: [Stage] → Pain: [What goes wrong]
Step 3: [Stage] → Pain: [What goes wrong]
Step 4: [Stage] → Pain: [What goes wrong]
Step 5: [Stage] → Pain: [What goes wrong]

Current solutions fall short because [gap]."
```

**Example:**
```
"For Digital Immigrants trying to connect with family on Facebook:

Step 1: Account creation → Pain: Too many fields, password requirements confusing
Step 2: Find family → Pain: Don't understand search, don't know how to send friend requests
Step 3: See updates → Pain: Feed algorithm shows ads/strangers, not family updates
Step 4: Engage (like, comment) → Pain: Tiny buttons, hard to tap, typos frustrate them
Step 5: Post own updates → Pain: Camera access confusing, don't know who sees posts (privacy anxiety)

Current solutions (GrandPad, Jitterbug) solve #1 but require family to also adopt. Facebook's advantage: family is already here. The pain is in steps 3-5 (engagement), not acquisition."
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- 4-6 journey steps
- Specific pain at each step (not vague "it's hard")
- Showed where current solutions fail
- Identified which pains are most critical

---

### Step 5: Judge & Prioritise Pain Points (2 minutes)

**What to do:**
Score each pain point using SCARU. Pick ONE.

**Structure:**
```
"Scoring pain points using SCARU:

Pain 1: [Description]
- Severity: [1-5] — [Why]
- Coverage: [1-5] — [% of users affected]
- Alignment: [1-5] — [Can we uniquely solve it?]
- Reality: [1-5] — [How confident in this signal?]
- Underserved: [1-5] — [Gap vs. competitors?]
- Score: [S × C × A × R × U]

Pain 2: [Description]
- [Same scoring]

Pain 3: [Description]
- [Same scoring]

Highest score: Pain X. I'll focus on [this pain]."
```

**Example:**
```
"Scoring pain points using SCARU:

Pain 1: Feed shows ads/strangers instead of family
- Severity: 4/5 — They came to see family, get spam instead
- Coverage: 5/5 — 100% of users experience this
- Alignment: 5/5 — Facebook controls the feed algorithm
- Reality: 4/5 — User research + my own grandparent interviews confirm
- Underserved: 5/5 — No competitor has "family-only feed"
- Score: 4 × 5 × 5 × 4 × 5 = 2,000

Pain 2: Tiny buttons hard to tap
- Severity: 3/5 — Annoying but not a blocker
- Coverage: 4/5 — 80% struggle with this
- Alignment: 5/5 — We control UI
- Reality: 5/5 — Well-documented accessibility issue
- Underserved: 3/5 — GrandPad solved this (bigger buttons)
- Score: 3 × 4 × 5 × 5 × 3 = 900

Pain 3: Privacy anxiety (who sees my posts)
- Severity: 5/5 — Paralyzing, stops them from posting
- Coverage: 3/5 — 60% worry about this
- Alignment: 4/5 — We control privacy settings, but hard to simplify
- Reality: 3/5 — Some evidence, but might be overstated
- Underserved: 4/5 — Others haven't solved elderly privacy UX
- Score: 5 × 3 × 4 × 3 × 4 = 720

Highest score: Pain 1 (Feed shows ads/strangers). I'll focus on making the feed family-centric."
```

**SCARU Scoring (1-5 scale each):**
- **S**everity: How painful? (5 = blocker, 1 = annoyance)
- **C**overage: What % affected? (5 = 100%, 1 = <20%)
- **A**lignment: Can THIS company solve it? (5 = unique advantage, 1 = no advantage)
- **R**eality: Confidence in signal? (5 = validated, 1 = assumption)
- **U**nderserved: Gap vs. competitors? (5 = no one solved it, 1 = well-addressed)

**Score = S × C × A × R (× U optional)**

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Scored 3+ pain points
- Used a framework (SCARU or similar)
- Showed the math
- Picked highest score (or explained deviation)
- Used judgment, not just formula

---

### Step 6: Propose Solutions (4 minutes)

**What to do:**
Generate 3 diverse solutions for the chosen pain point. Prioritize using EAC.

**Structure:**
```
"Three solutions for [Pain Point]:

Solution 1: [Name]
- What: [Description]
- How: [Key features]

Solution 2: [Name]
- What: [Description]
- How: [Key features]

Solution 3: [Name]
- What: [Description]
- How: [Key features]

Prioritizing using EAC:
- Effectiveness: [Which solves the problem best?]
- Alignment: [Which fits company mission/ecosystem best?]
- Cost/Effort: [Which is cheapest/fastest to build?]

Score = E × A / C

Solution [X] scores highest. Here's the detailed design:
[Go deep on chosen solution - 2 minutes]"
```

**Example:**
```
"Three solutions for 'Feed shows ads/strangers instead of family':

Solution 1: Family-Only Mode Toggle
- What: Button to switch feed to "Family Only" (filters out everything but family posts)
- How: User taps toggle, feed shows only posts from friends tagged as 'Family'

Solution 2: Smart Family Feed (AI-powered)
- What: ML model learns who user engages with, auto-prioritizes family
- How: Algorithm detects who user clicks on, messages, and surfaces their content first

Solution 3: Dedicated Family Tab
- What: Separate tab (like Groups, Marketplace) just for family
- How: User adds family members to "My Family" list, tab shows only their updates

Prioritizing using EAC:
- Effectiveness: Solution 3 is most effective (100% family, zero noise)
- Alignment: Solution 3 fits Facebook's mission AND existing tab structure
- Cost: Solution 1 is cheapest (simple filter). Solution 3 is medium (new tab + family list feature)

Scores:
- Solution 1: E=3, A=4, C=2 → 3×4/2 = 6
- Solution 2: E=4, A=3, C=5 → 4×3/5 = 2.4
- Solution 3: E=5, A=5, C=3 → 5×5/3 = 8.3

Solution 3 (Dedicated Family Tab) wins. Here's the design:

Family Tab:
- Lives in bottom nav (replace Marketplace for 70+ users)
- Onboarding: "Add your family" — search for children, grandchildren, siblings
- Feed shows: Photos, videos, life events from family list only
- Posting: "Share with family" — defaults to Family list (solves privacy anxiety)
- Features: Birthday reminders, family milestones, "call now" button for video chat

This solves the core pain (family signal lost in noise) AND the privacy pain (clear who sees posts)."
```

**EAC Scoring:**
- **E**ffectiveness: How well does it solve the problem? (1-5)
- **A**lignment: Fits company mission + ecosystem? (1-5)
- **C**ost/Effort: Time + resources to build? (1-5, higher = more expensive)

**Score = E × A / C** (maximize this)

**What evaluator looks for:**
- 3 distinct solutions (not variations of same idea)
- Used EAC or similar framework
- Showed the math
- Picked one with reasoning
- Went deep on chosen solution (features, user flow)
- Acknowledged trade-offs of others

---

### Step 7: Specify Success Metrics (2 minutes)

**What to do:**
Define North Star, input metrics, guardrails.

**Structure:**
```
"Success metrics:

North Star: [The ONE metric that proves this worked]
- Target: [Specific number]
- Why: [Why this metric matters]

Input Metrics: [What drives the North Star]
- [Metric 1]: [Target]
- [Metric 2]: [Target]
- [Metric 3]: [Target]

Guardrail Metrics: [What must not regress]
- [Metric 1]: [Threshold]
- [Metric 2]: [Threshold]
```

**Example:**
```
"Success metrics:

North Star: Weekly active family tab users (70+ age group)
- Target: 10M users within 12 months (20% of 50M US seniors on Facebook)
- Why: This measures adoption AND sustained usage (weekly = habit formed)

Input Metrics:
- Family list completion rate: 80% (users who add at least 3 family members)
- Family tab open rate: 60% of sessions (shows it's becoming primary use case)
- Family posts per week: 2 posts (shows comfort with sharing)

Guardrail Metrics:
- Overall Facebook MAU (70+): Must not decrease (can't cannibalize main product)
- Ad revenue per 70+ user: Must stay flat or grow (Family tab can still show some ads)
- Privacy complaints: <1% of Family tab users (ensure we didn't create new privacy issues)"
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- North Star is clear and measurable
- North Star directly reflects the pain point solved
- Input metrics are leading indicators (not lagging)
- Guardrails protect against unintended consequences
- Targets are specific (not "improve X")

---

### Step 8: Recommend (2 minutes)

**What to do:**
State recommendation with conviction. Summarize the logic. Acknowledge biggest risk.

**Structure:**
```
"My recommendation:

Build [Solution] for [Segment] because [Pain Point] is costing us [Opportunity Cost].

Why this works:
- Problem: [Restate the pain]
- Segment: [Who we're solving for]
- Solution: [What we're building]
- Unique advantage: [Why THIS company wins]

Biggest risk: [What could go wrong]
Mitigation: [How we de-risk]

This is the right bet because [core conviction]."
```

**Example:**
```
"My recommendation:

Build a dedicated Family Tab for Digital Immigrants (70-80 year olds) because they're coming to Facebook to see family but getting lost in ads and noise, and we're losing them to inactivity.

Why this works:
- Problem: Feed algorithm optimized for engagement, not family connection — seniors get overwhelmed
- Segment: 30M Digital Immigrants want to connect but current product is too noisy
- Solution: Family Tab gives them 100% signal, zero noise — family posts only
- Unique advantage: Facebook already HAS the family graph. Competitors (GrandPad, Jitterbug) require family to adopt a new platform. We just need to surface what we already have.

Biggest risk: Cannibalization. If seniors only use Family Tab, we lose ad impressions on main feed.
Mitigation: Family Tab can still show native ads (sponsored family milestones, senior-relevant products). And if this increases MAU, total ad inventory grows even if per-session inventory shrinks.

This is the right bet because 50M seniors on smartphones is the last major demographic shift in social, and we have a 3-year window before TikTok or YouTube figures out elderly UX. Facebook's competitive moat is the family graph — we need to activate it before someone else does."
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Stated recommendation clearly ("Build X for Y")
- Summarized the full logic (problem → segment → solution → advantage)
- Named the biggest risk
- Explained mitigation
- Showed conviction (not hedging)
- Tied back to strategic context from Step 1 (mission, competitive, market)

## Evaluation Rubric (Total: 10 points)

### Component 1: Mission & Intent (1 point)

**1 point (Excellent):**
- Named the company explicitly
- Quantified market size
- Mentioned industry trend
- Connected to company mission
- Stated unique advantage

**0.5 points (Partial):**
- Covered 3-4 of the above
- OR context present but shallow

**0 points (Missing):**
- Skipped strategic context
- Just said "big market" without numbers

---

### Component 2: Landscape & Segment (2 points)

**2 points (Excellent):**
- 3 segments defined by behavior (MECE)
- Used SFARU or similar prioritization framework
- Showed scoring/reasoning
- Picked ONE segment clearly
- Eliminated others explicitly

**1 point (Partial):**
- Segments defined but not MECE OR
- No framework used (just picked one) OR
- Weak reasoning

**0 points (Missing):**
- No segmentation
- Tried to solve for "all users"

---

### Component 3: Experience & Judge (2 points)

**2 points (Excellent):**
- Mapped 4-6 journey steps
- Pain points specific at each step
- Used SCARU or similar to prioritize pain
- Showed scoring math
- Picked ONE pain point

**1.5 points (Good):**
- Journey mapped but pain points vague OR
- Prioritized but no framework

**1 point (Partial):**
- Listed pains but no journey OR
- No prioritization (addressed multiple pains)

**0 points (Missing):**
- No user journey
- No pain points identified

---

### Component 4: Propose Solutions (3 points)

**3 points (Excellent):**
- 3 distinct solutions generated
- Used EAC or similar to prioritize
- Showed scoring math
- Picked one with reasoning
- Went deep on chosen solution (features, flow)
- Acknowledged trade-offs

**2 points (Good):**
- 2-3 solutions but similar (not diverse) OR
- Prioritized but no framework OR
- Shallow on solution detail

**1 point (Partial):**
- Only 1 solution OR
- No prioritization

**0 points (Missing):**
- No solution proposed
- Just said "we should improve X"

---

### Component 5: Success Metrics (1 point)

**1 point (Excellent):**
- North Star defined
- Input metrics named (2-3)
- Guardrails specified
- Targets are specific

**0.5 points (Partial):**
- North Star mentioned but no inputs OR
- Vague targets ("improve engagement")

**0 points (Missing):**
- No metrics

---

### Component 6: Recommend (1 point)

**1 point (Excellent):**
- Clear recommendation stated
- Summarized full logic (problem → solution → advantage)
- Named biggest risk + mitigation
- Showed conviction

**0.5 points (Partial):**
- Recommendation stated but weak summary OR
- No risk mentioned

**0 points (Missing):**
- No clear recommendation
- Hedged ("could do X or Y")

---

## Scoring Interpretation

**9-10 points:** Excellent. Hire strong.
- Strategic thinking + user research + prioritization + business sense
- Ready for senior PM role

**7-8 points:** Strong. Likely hire.
- Good product sense, minor gaps (e.g., weak on metrics or risk)
- Would succeed with coaching

**5-6 points:** Decent. Mixed signals.
- Either strong on user research but weak on business, OR vice versa
- Needs more practice

**3-4 points:** Weak. Likely no hire.
- Skipped key steps (no segmentation or no prioritization)
- Shallow thinking

**0-2 points:** Poor. No hire.
- Fundamental gaps in product thinking
- No structure

## Time Management Tips

**Total time: 20-25 minutes**

- **Mission**: 2 min
- **Industry**: 1 min (or skip if tight on time)
- **Landscape**: 3 min
- **Experience**: 3 min
- **Judge**: 2 min
- **Propose**: 4 min (1 min to list 3 solutions, 1 min to prioritize, 2 min deep dive on winner)
- **Success**: 2 min
- **Recommend**: 2 min

**If running out of time:**
- **Skip Industry** (nice-to-have context, not critical)
- **Compress Judge** (pick pain point with quick reasoning, don't show full SCARU math)
- **Keep Propose and Recommend** (these are the most important sections)

**If ahead of schedule:**
- Go deeper on solution design (mockups, edge cases, rollout phases)
- Add more detail to metrics (how you'd measure, what dashboards)

## Common Failure Modes to Watch For

### Failure Mode 1: No Segmentation
Tried to solve for "all users" instead of picking one segment.

**Feedback:** "You can't design for everyone. Next time: segment users by behavior, use SFARU to pick ONE, then design specifically for them."

---

### Failure Mode 2: Listed Pain Points Without Prioritization
Named 5 pain points and tried to solve all of them.

**Feedback:** "PMs must prioritize. You listed many pains but didn't pick ONE. Next time: use SCARU to score, then focus on the highest-impact pain."

---

### Failure Mode 3: Only One Solution
Generated one solution and didn't consider alternatives.

**Feedback:** "One solution suggests lack of creativity. Next time: generate 3 diverse solutions, prioritize with EAC, then go deep on the winner."

---

### Failure Mode 4: No Business Context
Jumped straight to user problems without strategic framing.

**Feedback:** "Products exist in a business context. Next time: start with Mission & Intent — why would THIS company build this? What's their unique advantage?"

---

### Failure Mode 5: No Metrics
Designed a solution but didn't say how to measure success.

**Feedback:** "How do you know if this worked? Next time: define North Star, input metrics, and guardrails."

---

### Failure Mode 6: Weak Recommendation
Ended with "so yeah, I think we should build this" (no conviction).

**Feedback:** "Recommendations need conviction. Next time: summarize the full logic (problem → solution → advantage), name the biggest risk, and show why this is THE right bet."

## Framework Adaptation

### For "Improve an existing product" questions

**Adjust these steps:**

**Mission & Intent:**
- Why improve this NOW? What changed?
- What's the strategic priority? (Growth? Monetization? Retention?)

**Landscape & Segment:**
- Segment existing users (not potential users)
- Prioritize which segment to improve for

**Experience & Judge:**
- Map current product experience
- Identify where it falls short

Rest of framework stays the same.

---

### For "Your favorite product" questions

This framework is **NOT** designed for favorite product questions. Use a different structure:
1. What the product is
2. Why you love it (personal connection)
3. What it does well (3 things)
4. What you'd improve (1-2 things)
5. Why it's successful in market

---

## Critical Reminders

**MILEJPSR is comprehensive but flexible:**
- **Short on time?** Skip Industry (I), compress Judge (J)
- **Design question?** Focus on L-E-J-P (user research + solution design)
- **Improve question?** Focus on E-J-P (experience, pain, solution)

**Prioritization is mandatory:**
- You MUST pick ONE segment (don't solve for all users)
- You MUST pick ONE pain point (don't solve all pains)
- You MUST pick ONE solution (don't say "we could do A or B")

**Show the frameworks:**
- When you use SFARU, show the scoring
- When you use SCARU, show the scoring
- When you use EAC, show the scoring
- Interviewers want to see structured thinking, not just "I picked this"

**Conviction matters:**
- Don't hedge in your recommendation
- Don't say "we could try this and see"
- Say "This IS the right bet because [conviction]"
