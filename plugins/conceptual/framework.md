# Definition-Example Framework for Conceptual Questions

## What This Framework Is

The Definition-Example Framework is designed for "What's the difference between X and Y?" or "Explain [Concept]" questions where you need to show conceptual clarity and practical application.

**Framework Steps:**
1. **Define** each term clearly
2. **Distinguish** - state the key difference
3. **Example** - give concrete illustration
4. **When to Use** - explain application

---

## When to Use This Framework

**Use for questions like:**
- "What's the difference between strategy and roadmap?"
- "Explain network effects"
- "North Star metric vs. OKR — when would you use each?"
- "What makes a good product vision?"

**Core skill being tested:**
Do you understand PM concepts deeply? Can you explain complex ideas simply? Can you apply theory to practice?

---

## What This Framework Tests

### Conceptual Clarity
- Do you understand the core concept?
- Can you define it without jargon?
- Do you avoid circular definitions?

### Communication
- Can you explain it simply?
- Do you structure your explanation logically?
- Do you avoid over-complicating?

### Practical Application
- Can you give concrete examples?
- Do you show when to use one vs. the other?
- Do you connect theory to real product decisions?

### Structured Thinking
- Do you follow a clear structure?
- Do you build from definitions → distinction → example → application?
- Is your reasoning easy to follow?

## The Four Steps (5 minutes total)

### Step 1: Define Each Term (60 seconds)

**What to do:**
Define each term clearly, without using jargon.

**Structure:**
```
"Let me define each term:

[Term A]: [Definition in plain English]

[Term B]: [Definition in plain English]
```

**Good definition checklist:**
- ✅ Plain English (no jargon)
- ✅ Complete (captures the essence)
- ✅ Not circular (doesn't use the term to define itself)
- ✅ Concise (1-2 sentences)

**Example for "Strategy vs. Roadmap":**
```
"Let me define each:

Strategy: The high-level plan for achieving a goal. It answers: What are we trying to accomplish? Why does it matter? How will we win?

Roadmap: The time-sequenced plan for executing the strategy. It answers: What will we build? When will we build it? In what order?"
```

**Bad definitions to avoid:**
- ❌ "Strategy is strategic direction" (circular)
- ❌ "A roadmap is like a map for your product" (metaphor, not definition)
- ❌ "Strategy involves strategic planning to achieve strategic goals" (jargon)

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Both terms defined
- Definitions are clear and concise
- No circular reasoning
- No jargon (or jargon explained)

---

### Step 2: State the Key Distinction (60 seconds)

**What to do:**
In one sentence, state the core difference.

**Structure:**
```
"The key distinction:

[Term A] is about [X], while [Term B] is about [Y].

Another way to think about it:
[Term A] answers [Question 1], [Term B] answers [Question 2]."
```

**Example:**
```
"The key distinction:

Strategy is about WHAT and WHY, while Roadmap is about WHEN and HOW.

Another way to think about it:
Strategy answers 'Where are we going?' and 'Why is that the right direction?'
Roadmap answers 'What do we build first?' and 'When do we ship it?'"
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- One clear sentence stating the difference
- Not just "they're different" but HOW they're different
- Ideally uses a dimension (what vs when, qualitative vs quantitative, etc.)

---

### Step 3: Give an Example (90 seconds)

**What to do:**
Pick a real product and show what each term looks like in practice.

**Structure:**
```
"Example with [Product]:

[Term A] for [Product]:
[Concrete example of the concept]

[Term B] for [Product]:
[Concrete example of the concept]

Notice how [Term A] is [X], while [Term B] is [Y]."
```

**Example:**
```
"Example with Instagram Commerce:

Strategy for Instagram Commerce:
'Own the full transaction, not just discovery. We're leaking $100B in GMV to Amazon because we don't have checkout. Our strategy is to become a commerce destination, not just an ad platform.'

Roadmap for Instagram Commerce:
'Year 1: InstaBuy checkout (launch Q2, scale Q4). Year 2: Creator storefronts (Q1 beta, Q3 full launch). Year 3: AI shopping assistant (Q2 pilot).'

Notice how the strategy is STABLE (doesn't change quarterly) and explains the business case, while the roadmap is FLEXIBLE (adjusts based on learnings) and shows specific products with timelines."
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Concrete example from a real product
- Example illustrates the distinction clearly
- Not just made-up hypothetical ("imagine a company that...")

---

### Step 4: When to Use Each (60 seconds)

**What to do:**
Explain in what situations you'd use one vs. the other.

**Structure:**
```
"When to use [Term A]:
[Situation 1]
[Situation 2]

When to use [Term B]:
[Situation 1]
[Situation 2]

A common mistake: [Misconception to avoid]"
```

**Example:**
```
"When to use Strategy:
- Explaining to executives or board why we're making this bet
- Aligning the team on the big picture before diving into details
- Making trade-offs (should we focus on monetization or growth?)

When to use Roadmap:
- Planning sprints and quarterly goals
- Communicating to eng/design what to build next
- Tracking progress (are we on schedule?)

A common mistake: Confusing a roadmap with a strategy. Saying 'our strategy is to ship features A, B, C' is not a strategy—that's a roadmap. Strategy must explain WHY those features matter for the business."
```

**What evaluator looks for:**
- Practical situations for each
- Shows understanding of when one is appropriate vs. the other
- Bonus: mentions a common misconception

## Evaluation Rubric (Total: 10 points)

### Component 1: Define Each Term (2 points)

**2 points (Excellent):**
- Both terms defined clearly
- Plain English (no jargon)
- Not circular
- Concise (1-2 sentences each)

**1 point (Partial):**
- Definitions attempted but unclear
- OR too abstract/jargony
- OR circular reasoning

**0 points (Missing):**
- Didn't define both terms
- Definitions are wrong

---

### Component 2: State Key Distinction (3 points)

**3 points (Excellent):**
- One clear sentence stating the difference
- Explained HOW they differ (what vs when, etc.)
- Not just "they're different"
- Used a dimension/framework to organize the distinction

**2 points (Good):**
- Stated the difference but less precisely
- Somewhat vague ("one is more strategic")

**1 point (Partial):**
- Mentioned they're different but didn't explain how

**0 points (Missing):**
- Couldn't articulate the distinction
- Said "they're kind of the same"

---

### Component 3: Give Example (3 points)

**3 points (Excellent):**
- Concrete example from real product
- Example clearly illustrates both concepts
- Shows the distinction in practice

**2 points (Good):**
- Example given but somewhat generic
- OR example doesn't clearly show the distinction

**1 point (Partial):**
- Hypothetical example only ("imagine a company...")
- OR example is too vague

**0 points (Missing):**
- No example
- All theory, no application

---

### Component 4: When to Use (2 points)

**2 points (Excellent):**
- Explained when to use each
- Practical situations given
- Mentioned a common misconception (bonus)

**1 point (Partial):**
- Some guidance but vague
- OR only covered one term

**0 points (Missing):**
- Didn't explain when to use each

---

## Scoring Interpretation

**9-10 points:** Excellent. Crystal clear understanding.
- Can explain complex concepts simply
- Practical application strong
- Ready for senior PM role

**7-8 points:** Strong. Good conceptual grasp.
- Minor gaps (e.g., example could be stronger)
- Understands the concepts well

**5-6 points:** Decent. Basic understanding.
- Definitions OK but distinction weak
- OR good theory but no practical application

**3-4 points:** Weak. Shaky understanding.
- Circular definitions
- Couldn't articulate distinction
- No examples

**0-2 points:** Poor. Doesn't understand.
- Wrong definitions
- Confused concepts

## Common Failure Modes to Watch For

### Failure Mode 1: Circular Definitions
Defined the term using the term itself.

**Example:** "Strategy is the strategic approach to achieving strategic goals."

**Feedback:** "Your definition is circular. Define strategy WITHOUT using the word 'strategic'. Next time: use plain English."

---

### Failure Mode 2: No Distinction
Said "they're different" but couldn't explain HOW.

**Example:** "Strategy and roadmap are both important but different."

**Feedback:** "You said they're different but didn't explain the distinction. Next time: What dimension separates them? (What vs when? High-level vs detailed?)"

---

### Failure Mode 3: No Examples
All theory, no practice.

**Feedback:** "I understand the theory but can't picture it in practice. Next time: pick a real product (Instagram, Spotify, Notion) and show what each concept looks like."

---

### Failure Mode 4: Over-Complicating
Used jargon to hide lack of understanding.

**Example:** "Strategy is the synergistic alignment of organizational imperatives to optimize value creation pathways."

**Feedback:** "Too complicated. If you can't explain it to a smart 12-year-old, you don't understand it. Next time: use plain English."

---

### Failure Mode 5: Wrong Answer
Fundamentally misunderstood the concepts.

**Example:** "A roadmap is just a list of features."

**Feedback:** "That's not accurate. A roadmap is time-sequenced (Year 1 vs 2 vs 3) and shows prioritization logic, not just a list."

## Time Management Tips

**Total time: 5 minutes**

- **60 sec**: Define both terms
- **60 sec**: State distinction
- **90 sec**: Give example
- **60 sec**: When to use each
- **30 sec**: Buffer

**If running out of time:**
- Skip "When to Use" section
- Make sure you hit Define + Distinction + Example (core 3)

**If ahead of schedule:**
- Add a second example
- Mention a common misconception
- Connect to a decision you made in your past role

## Example Concepts & Key Distinctions

To help you prepare, here are common concept pairs and their key distinctions:

### Strategy vs. Roadmap
**Distinction:** Strategy = WHAT and WHY (stable). Roadmap = WHEN and HOW (flexible).

### Mission vs. Vision
**Distinction:** Mission = purpose (why we exist). Vision = destination (what the world looks like if we succeed).

### North Star vs. OKR
**Distinction:** North Star = THE metric that represents customer value. OKR = quarterly goals to drive the North Star.

### Product-Market Fit vs. Product-Channel Fit
**Distinction:** PMF = product solves a real problem (people want it). PCF = you found the right distribution channel (you can reach them).

### Platform vs. Marketplace
**Distinction:** Platform = enables others to build (API, SDK). Marketplace = matches buyers and sellers (two-sided network).

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicator
**Distinction:** Leading = predicts future outcome (signup flow conversion). Lagging = measures past outcome (revenue).

### B2B vs. B2B2C
**Distinction:** B2B = sell to businesses, they use it. B2B2C = sell to businesses, they distribute to consumers.

### MVP vs. MLP
**Distinction:** MVP = Minimum Viable Product (test if it works). MLP = Minimum Lovable Product (test if people love it).

### 0→1 vs. 1→10
**Distinction:** 0→1 = create something new (search for PMF). 1→10 = scale something proven (optimize for growth).

### Horizontal vs. Vertical (SaaS)
**Distinction:** Horizontal = serves many industries (Slack, Notion). Vertical = serves one industry deeply (Veeva for pharma).

## Framework Adaptation

**For "Explain [Concept]" questions** (not comparison):

Use this structure:
1. **Define**: What it is
2. **Why It Matters**: Why this concept exists
3. **Example**: Concrete illustration
4. **When to Use**: Practical application

**Example: "Explain network effects"**

```
Define: Network effects occur when a product becomes more valuable as more people use it.

Why It Matters: Creates defensibility. Competitors can't easily replicate a strong network (see Facebook, LinkedIn).

Example: LinkedIn is more valuable with 1B professionals than 100K. More users → more connections → more value per user → more users (flywheel).

When to Use: When designing products with social, marketplace, or platform dynamics. NOT for single-player products (Netflix content is valuable regardless of how many subscribers).
```
